This article reflects on the psychological burden of "helping" through the author's internship experience. The author finds themselves repeatedly taking on responsibilities that aren't theirs, leading to feelings of frustration. The story highlights how the author learns to set boundaries, realizing they have the right to say "no" and maintain inner balance. In the end, the author discovers that rejecting requests doesn't always cause conflict, as long as it's done with clarity and respect.

---

Mr. Wang had been the top student in the city's college entrance exams, and the ladies in the office whispered about it when he wasn't around.

"You know, he seemed to be the top student in the city college entrance exam at that time."  
"So powerful, then why is he here?"  
"We don't know, it's rumored that the school went through a lot of trouble to keep him here."  
"What happened to his leg? How can he sit and lecture?"  
"I don't know, every day we have to let people carry him up and down the stairs, you see him go to the toilet is not fine."  

I was silent, busy with my own things, but for this man I helped him bring food every day, carried up and downstairs, can not help but feel curious, but then this curiosity into boredom, in fact, more of my own boredom.

At the beginning of March, I went to a town elementary school in Weihai with a few junior students from the same school for an internship, and I was assigned to the first grade office, where I crossed paths with Mr. Wang. It was the day he went home on leave, and I was to temporarily replace him for the next day, teaching two classes of math, labor, science, and study hall, roughly 14 lessons a week, which was the closest I'd ever been to the first graders.

The nightmare began one day a week later. On his first day back from vacation, I was called to carry him upstairs with three other physical education teachers. That afternoon, the only people left in the office were me and the first grade division head, who happened to double as the gym teacher, and it seemed only natural that I would be asked to carry him down the stairs. It didn't bother me at first, but as time went on, I gradually felt that it had somehow become my responsibility. Although it was not explicitly requested or directed, it seemed to come naturally to me as something I should do. If he had been indispensable to my work, I might not have been so troubled. But the fact is that there is hardly any communication between us.

The thing that really bugged me was something that happened one lunchtime. At that time, Mr. Wang and I were the only ones left in the office, and I was about to go to lunch when he stopped me and asked me to bring him lunch. Before that, he and his colleagues who had been working around him for the whole year had not asked for it, and in favor of asking me, an intern, to help? I didn't refuse or say anything, but at that moment, I felt like I was being kidnapped by something called "morality". He pressured me directly: "If you don't bring it, I'll have to go hungry." I realized that my choice was no longer a choice.

The boredom came from a mental imbalance. I wanted to be rewarded for my efforts, even if it was a simple thank you or a profound conversation that made a difference. However, nothing came back for a long time, which made me feel disappointed. Every time I walked through the corridor, I saw Mr. Wang sitting peacefully at the podium lecturing, and the ladies in the office talking, all of which continued to intensify my sense of boredom.

To make matters worse, since Mr. Wang couldn't make it to the cafeteria, he needed someone to bring him food, and that person eventually turned out to be me. At first, it was my guidance counselor and the language teacher in the office who helped. But I forget how the matter became my responsibility, probably because I helped once, and after that there was a second and a third time, until countless times ...... Finally, at one point, I got impatient and tried to shrug it off, but they seemed to think that I should continue to help out every time the next time, and as a result, the matter just went on again and again and again went on and on.

Eventually, time helped me to end it all. Four months into my internship, I was finally able to get rid of responsibilities that did not belong to me. These responsibilities came about because of my own mental deficiencies. It was only this year that I began to pay attention to these psychological phenomena. Before this year, due to my single relationship with people around me, I did not have the opportunity to encounter similar scenarios, nor did I realize these psychological defects. This internship was a deep reflection on myself.

Why did I not dare to say "no" at that time? As a matter of fact, I should have been qualified to say "no" whether or not there was a reason, even if it was simply that I did not want to do something for someone else, and there was no need to have any reason to refuse Mr. Wang, the supervisor, or the language teacher. Obviously, I didn't do it at that time. I thought too much about complex situations and was always looking for an outlet to avoid conflict. Conflict was a nightmare for me, and I always tried to avoid it. However, when you think about it, rejection doesn't always lead to conflict. As long as it's worded properly, you don't need any excuses when rejecting someone. However, my subconscious mind has always believed that rejection must lead to conflict, and this thought is so deeply rooted in my mind that I have only today begun to realize the distress it causes. It limits my space to make choices and makes me accept responsibilities that are not in my favor, and this unequal exchange relationship inevitably leads to an imbalance in the psychological and life order.

Rejection does not necessarily bring about conflict, the key lies in how one expresses oneself. Today, I am more comfortable making choices that are in line with my heart and avoid getting caught up in unnecessary responsibilities and psychological burdens.
